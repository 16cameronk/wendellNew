Bio

Wendell Nii Laryea Adjetey is a doctoral candidate in the Departments of History and African American Studies at Yale University. He received the M.A. and M.Phil. in May 2015 and will earn the Ph.D. in May 2018. He holds numerous awards, prizes, and distinctions, including Yale’s Felix G. Evangelist, Douglass R. Bomeisler, Edla J. McPherson, and Falk Foundation Fellowships. He is a beneficiary of a Pierre Elliott Trudeau Foundation Doctoral Scholarship, Canada’s most prestigious doctoral award for the humanities and social sciences. He has held a Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council (SSHRC) of Canada Doctoral Fellowship. In 2016-2017, he studied as a Visiting Scholar and Senior Resident Fellow at Massey College, University of Toronto. Adjetey earned an undergraduate degree in history and political science in 2008 and an M.A. in 2009 in political science and ethnic, immigration, and pluralism studies from the University of Toronto. Currently, he is a Visiting Scholar and Pre-Doctoral Fellow in the History Section at MIT.

Adjetey is an honorary New Englander through residence, Canadian via naturalization, and Ghanaian by birth.

Research

A historian of the United States and Canada, Adjetey’s research interests include: Social and Cultural History, African American History, Chattel Slavery in the Black Atlantic, Black Loyalists, Afrodiasporic Urban Communities, post-Reconstruction Black Labor, Immigration, Black Internationalism, and Civil and Human Rights. His doctoral research integrates themes such as Black Nationalism, immigration and settlement, civil rights and human rights, and post-war white backlash in Canada and the United States. Entitled “From the North Star to the Black Star: African North Americans and the Search for a Homeland in Canada, 1919 to 1985,” his project illuminates the advent of race consciousness, persistence of cross-border migration, and emergence and divergence of a post-war human rights regime in Canada and civil rights in the United States. His work bridges antebellum and Reconstruction black struggles in nineteenth-century North America to African American and African Canadian aspirations for citizenship in the twentieth-century. He has begun research on the histories of deportation in North America and African American (self-)exile.
